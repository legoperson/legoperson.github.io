Option 1

The Australian Property Technology CRC (PropTech CRC) is a body ensuring that focused, industry-driven collaboration between academia, governments and the PropTech industry is enabled over the next decade to transform Australias residential property markets. Together with our partners we will drive the creation of technology solutions that effect real change across the property life cycle: solutions that unlock the connectivity, access, supply and capital required to address Australias housing crisis. The PropTech CRC will seek to create the modern property markets, that facilitates competition to deliver solutions that create a better property market experience for Australian owners, renters and investors to establish the global benchmark for the worlds largest asset class and enable partners to export their solutions globally. The PropTech CRC will also accelerate Australias progress toward hitting key Government targets in the building of 1.2m well-located homes over the next five years and in having 1.2m Australians working in the tech sector by 2030.[1]

Option 2

The Australian Property Technology CRC (PropTech CRC) will exploit the opportunities arising from the transformation of the real estate sector through the digitisation and interoperability between entities in the property life cycle. This body will drive the creation of a modern, competitive property market, setting a global benchmark and enabling role for partners to establish the digital rails required to export their solutions worldwide. The technological solutions will delivery efficiency gains to unlock connectivity, access, supply, and capital across the property lifecycle with the aim of addressing roadblocks in housing affordability. The PropTech CRC will also support Australias goal of building more than 1.2 million well-located homes in the next five years through R&D, piloting, and commercialising solutions. Beyond physical and technological solutions the PropTech CRC will enhance industry skills, and develop leadership capabilities boosting the tech sector workforce beyond to 1.2 million Australians.

Real estate is Australias largest asset class, valued at $10 trillion: more than 3x Australias superannuation holdings. For many Australians, a house is the most important purchase theyll ever make, and housing security is critical: without it, health, welfare and economic productivity suffer significantly.

Yet, it is well known that Australias residential property markets are not working. Rental vacancies are at an all-time low as renters face record rents. Ownership pathways are failing as rising prices outpace traditional savings methods. Inefficient planning processes restrict new supply and lead to excessive holding costs. Potential buyers can only purchase entire houses or units, meaning investors compete directly with occupiers, while searching for and transacting property lacks transparency.[2]

There are several reasons for this, but key is the fact that Australias residential real estate markets are not part of a coordinated, end-to-end system but rather various rules and actors. These include planning authorities, developers, financiers, builders and builders, and each has limited integration and visibility. Coordination across these groups is lacking, leaving them unable to collaborate to solve problems and that ordinary Australians suffer as a result.

The Government has recently made housing a key priority through the Housing Australia Future Fund and other initiatives. Yet, policy initiatives alone are not enough: Australia needs efforts that connect and streamline the entire residential property lifecycle. While there are islands of digitisation and automation emerging in the PropTech sector, these are fragmented and in need of integration. No party is capable of connecting all the actors involved to drive real change as they would not be able to capture the full benefits investment.

The PropTech CRC is perfectly positioned to achieve this. It will build the essential digital rail that links together industry, government, researchers and communities. It will drive the collaboration and coordination required to create the arrangements that transform Australias residential property markets and unlock significant opportunities for innovation and economic growth.

A CRC is the right vehicle to ensure success in this area because:

         The scale and urgency of the problem requires coordinated innovation and an independent research driven approach

         The disparate nature of stakeholders required to achieve real transformation requires a long-term, collaborative effort[3]

         Key problems to be solved are multidisciplinary, requiring a combination of deep industry expertise across the entire residential property lifecycle, technology innovation (especially AI and data-driven approaches), expertise in financial services and market design, as well as law, engineering and sustainability

Together with our partners, the CRC is focused on researching and developing the solutions that tackle the full residential property lifecycle. At the heart of our bid is an R&D program uniting leading researchers across design, urban planning, property economics, law and technology with industry partners in PropTech, finance, technology, SME innovators, and with Government and the not-for-profit housing sector. These programs will be supplemented by an education and training program that delivers highly trained human capital, positioning Australia as a global leader in the PropTech industry and in solving global housing challenges. Noting that access to adequate, safe and affordable housing is explicitly a global target as outlined in the United National Sustainable Development Goal (SDG 11) Sustainable Cities and Communities.

Our integrated and targeted Research Programs stem 4 pillars to deliver maximum impact:

(1)   Unlocking Access addresses the property transaction experience to tackle to inflexible and suboptimal ways that owners, buyers, renters and investors rent/lease and buy/sell property. It aims to make it easier to get millions of Australians into appropriate housing and improve housing security and affordability particularly for essential workers

(2)   Unlocking Supply deepens and extends promising efforts to use digital technologies that support urban planning and decision making with the aim of removing supply bottlenecks and reducing time from design to sale as well as better using existing housing stock

(3)   Unlocking Capital addresses the current underutilisation of institutional capital and private finance to support a fairer, greener housing market[4]

(4)   Unlocking Connectivity develops the platforms and interoperable data solutions that better connect actors across Australias residential property sector: the technological foundations through which ecosystem participants can collaborate and rapidly test solutions that innovate throughout the entire property lifecycle.

The solutions created by these programs will accelerate new housing supply, create innovative and more transparent ways for Australians to find, save for and purchase property, and position Australia as a world leader in PropTech. They will deliver new jobs through driving industry growth and create new services, businesses and technologies all while getting millions of Australians into homes and boosting housing security.

THE CRISIS

Housing security is fundamental to the effective functioning of society. Yet, Australia is currently experiencing a housing crisis one which shows no signs of abating. Renters face record-low vacancies and record-high rents. Purchasing a property is unattainable for many, while the nation faces shortfalls in supply particularly in social and affordable housing. This crisis has severe implications for Australian health and wellbeing, economic productivity and competitiveness, and even the social fabric of the country. As such the Government has recently made housing a key priority: a push best evidenced by several initiatives such as the recent creation of the Housing Australia Future Fund and the announcement of ambitious new housing targets. At the same time, the both the Minister for Housing and the CEO of Housing Australia have actively called for private sector cooperation in the creation of solutions.[5]

Two factor problem Real estate is backward; wider context also a brake on Australian productivity

THE PROBLEM

There is willingness on the part of the private residential property sector to make a difference in this space.[6] Significant improvements can be made through leveraging new Property Technology (PropTech) solutions involving digitisation, automation and AI.[7] To this end, 80% of residential real estate actors plan to invest heavily in technology and R&D over the next three years. However, extensive consultation with industry has made it clear that the sector is facing three fundamental, interrelated constraints that must be overcome before this innovation can be effectively leveraged to solve Australias housing crisis. These constraints cannot be tackled without the creation of the PropTech CRC.[8]

The first is one of scale and complexity. Australias residential real estate actors are not part of a coordinated, end-to-end system but a loosely connected collection of markets marked by significant inefficiencies, asymmetries and fragmentation. This ecosystem contains a plethora of different actors, such as planning authorities (both state and municipality level), developers, financiers, builders and buyers/renters. Each group has limited integration and visibility across different domains, each has different forms of interaction, many compete with one another, and, finally, each has different needs and priorities. As such, coordination across these groups is lacking, ensuring that no actor is properly able or incentivised to collaborate and solve the issues confronting our country.

Second is poor data standards and protocols. Data is the lifeblood of modern developed economies, and yet Australian real estate data are fragmented, limited by interoperability issues and generally collected from admin systems rather than purpose-built processes. Consultations have made it clear that the sector lacks agreement on basic definitions and standards that are required for data to flow, making it harder to provide transparency and basic information about Australian real estate as well as plan for the future. This limits sectoral ability to innovate and test new solutions, understand and mitigate climate risk, and in the context of boosting housing supply, harder to for investors to invest in new projects. Recognising this challenge, several industry players in conjunction with the National Housing Supply and Affordability Council have called for the creation of a group to promote the development of interoperable databases that could facilitate large scale investment in Australian real estate and boost innovation across the sector.[9]

Taken together these issues have prevented the sector from undergoing the type of digital transformation now the norm in the 21st century. Many industries globally are now marked by a series of key digital processes and systems that connect different sector participants even between competing parties and the emergence of these building blocks has transformed various sectors, resulting in significant transparency, agility and transformational goods and services. By contrast, Australias residential property ecosystem is marked by the prevalence of traditional methods and approaches, leaving it plagued by replication, inefficiencies, and, in many cases, a desire to defend my patch. This is directly limiting industry competitiveness, potential to grow both locally and internationally, and capacity to solve the housing crisis.[10]

TRANSFORMATIONAL R&D

The PropTech CRC is perfectly positioned to address these challenges and enable the transformation of Australias $10 trn residential real estate ecosystem. Our group of industry and academic partners represent key stakeholder groups across the entire real estate lifecycle partners who have made clear their willingness to work together with the CRC to facilitate real change.[11]

This effort will centre on our transformational R&D program, which is founded on the tried-and-true CRC model: connecting academic expertise with industry experience and world class education & training to achieve outcomes otherwise not possible. The R&D program has been developed through extensive consultation with our partners and has two broad goals. The first is encapsulated by our stream unlock connectivity: to build and innovate the enabling technologies that drive Australias real estate markets into the 21st century. This involves creating digital rails: the trust and technologies that connect disparate actors across the ecosystem, developing and implementing key fit-for-purpose data standards and systems to ensure effective cross-sectoral data flows, and in turn, facilitating the foundational digital transformation required by the sector.

This will serve as the gateway for our second goal: turbocharging Australian PropTech innovation. To this end, our other three research streams will focus on developing and accelerating technologies that tackle the residential property lifecycle and remove frictions associated with planning, purchasing, and investing at scale in housing all to unlock greater access for Australians, improve housing supply, and upscale the deployment of positive capital in Australian real estate. Indeed, there is significant potential for this innovation to tackle many of the key issues inherent in the housing crisis, such as (but not limited to):

         Digitising and applying AI to planning rules/codes promises material reductions in delays caused by processes which are currently outdated, uncertain, and vary greatly across jurisdictions. This will improve the speed at which new developments can enter the market and boost supply.

         New data platforms providing transparency on e.g. home valuation, construction and maintenance histories can radically reduce information asymmetries between buyer/seller and landlord/renter, reducing costs, delays and uncertainty and giving Australians greater confidence in home choices.

         The acceleration of innovative financial mechanisms such as real-estate index linked notes can help Australians save for and purchase new homes that are otherwise out of reach due to rapid rises in house prices.

         Institutional capital can play a key role in alleviating supply pressure by funding new projects but is currently heavily-underutilised: <1% of superfund real estate investments are in Australian residential. Connected data provision, precise valuation models and planning tools can allow funds to invest with greater confidence.

         New data-enabled tools can empower planners and authorities to engage in advanced scenario planning and rapidly decide with confidence on where and when to build in real time to avoid dumb density developments that do not meet local demographic needs or that are not served by appropriate infrastructure.

         Traditional building techniques are no longer fit for purpose in the 21st century.[12] Modular design and industrial construction techniques are required to supply stock faster into market. This can be effectively enabled through digitising housing assets and creating digital twins that provide developers, financers and policymakers with the information required to see ensure supply effectively meets demand.

VALUE ADD

The creation of the PropTech CRC represents a clear value add over existing industry approaches. Industry cannot solve problems facing the ecosystem without a trusted, cross-sectoral body like the CRC. Industry participants currently have limited incentives to coordinate and solve sectoral constraints: data is commercially sensitive, while there are clear first mover disadvantages any party taking action to enable the sector will not be able to capture the full benefits of their investment. Nor does any single player have the visibility or scale to make real impact alone.

As stated above, actors across the ecosystem are looking to invest heavily in new technologies and solutions. Yet the nature of the constraints facing the sector are such that, if the status quo continues, this investment will simply lead to more fragmentation and blockages and potentially the creation of cottage industries dealing in limited products. Rather than expanding the number of solutions that can grow the industry and create real impact, this risks deepening the replication and inefficiencies across the sector.

By contrast, effective action is uniquely enabled by the CRC model, which fosters the long-term partnerships required between different actors to achieve real impact. The CRC and our partners will define technology standards and platforms required to enable the sector and drive these across Australia. This in turn will turbocharge innovation and unlock value far beyond what is possible under existing arrangements, while enhancing sectoral capacity to adapt to new challenges and integrate future technologies ensuring that the sector is well positioned for sustainable growth and future innovation and giving Australian firms strong market advantages globally. This will also lead to an enormous value add for Australia. Enabling the sector will lead to solutions that make a real impact on Australias housing crisis and get more Australians into appropriate housing something which in itself will also bring significant positive spillover effects to Australin industries in general.

APPROACH AND METHODOLOGIES

Driving the change necessary to transform Australias residential ecosystem requires an interdisciplinary approach. It requires collaboration across numerous stakeholder participants and the uniting of deep industry expertise with specialists across a range of academic disciplines, including economics, finance, law, urban planning, urban sciences, social sciences, design and architecture. Such an effort needs to be underpin by information and technology expertise in AI, data science, human centred design, system, database design and visualisation. To this end, the CRC has assembled experts from its network of X universities. They include X professors many of whom are recognised as global leaders in their fields and frequently cited in Government reviews and senate inquiries, and one researcher who sits on the National Housing Council.

Our research program has been informed by extensive consultation with participants across both industry and academia. Doing so has seen us identify four key research pillars that cannot be solved by specific actors from industry, government or academia alone, and that represent opportunities to drive real change for Australians and provide industry partners with strong market advantages in both local and global contexts. These four pillars centre on unlocking impact across the full breadth of the residential real estate cycle: unlock access, unlock supply, unlock capital, and unlock connectivity.

These streams target collaborative and focused R&D that scales and develops existing instances of innovation in the Australian PropTech ecosystem, unlocks entirely new solutions and commercial opportunities, and that produces maximum real-world impact in positive timeframes. As such, each stream is designed for rapidly developing, piloting and evaluating solutions through a human centre design approach for deeply engaging partners in capturing their expertise and requirements under the coordination of the PropTech CRC.

Central to the execution of research projects is the utilisation of data and technology platforms that provide environments for the development and testing of solutions. These digital environments will utilise partner data whilst respecting IP and commercially insensitive information through specific data linkage and obfuscation techniques. Furthermore, cutting edge research and hypothesis testing frequently involves trial and error as an integral part of the R&D process. As such, operational R&D environments are fully partitioned from partner core business operations but at the same time remain directly aligned with core business activities. This allows successful R&D projects and outcomes to be rapidly implemented and integrated in real-world commercial contexts. A cloud based microservices architecture will underpin the development of pilot and proof of concepts applications. Such an architecture will support a federated approach to the development of innovative data and technology products that can then be rapidly plug and played with commercial solution to test their viability in the real-world. Such an approach will support the rapid commercialisation of new digital housing data and analytical services into the market.

The sheer complexity of Australias housing challenge means that R&D and solutions developed in one specific stream may have profound impacts in the domain covered by another stream. For this reason, continuous collaboration is crucial. As such, streams will engage in regular dialogue and insight sharing through both structured (symposia, hackathons and sprints, and seminars) and unstructured formats (e.g. informal discussion in office/academic settings). It is important to note that any partner IP and confidential material will be protected to the highest standards during said collaboration through appropriate safeguards and policies including through implementation of the Secure-by-Design approach for the development of digital products and services .

 

ADDRESSING IDENTIFIED PROBLEMS, BUILDING ON CURRENT BODY OF KNOWLEDGE and ENHANCING ADOPTION OF NEW TECHNOLOGIES

 

Australia is facing a growing housing crisis, exasperated by issues of market failure in housing supply and demand and related impacts on housing affordability and basic access to social and community housing stock. At the same time Australias population growth is projected to increase by 15 million people of the next 40 years (Treasury, 2023). Thus, the demand for housing will significantly increase and is both a long-term challenge and opportunity.

In order to plan for future housing decision-makers across government and industry need access to accurate and timely data and analytics. In Australia a myriad of data and technology solutions do exist at various levels of maturity. However, there is the significant problem of information asymmetry Akerlof (1970) and data fragmentation in regarding to housing market outcomes (e.g. Loosemore et al. 2019). If we look at housing data assets, these are highly fragmented (siloed) across government agencies and the private sector with different levels of access and restrictions on use for research and decision-making purposes.

State governments have a series of e-planning portals which are there to assist with the assessment and approval of planning applications to build future housing stock. Yet such platforms are experiencing significant difficulties in use and do not easily interoperate with other digital platforms such as Industry partners Archistars Property Platform or PEXAs Value Australias Automated Valuations platform. Furthermore, the data standards and across jurisdictions are inconsistent and require interoperability and harmonisation solutions. The PropTech CRC will provide the necessary digital rails so solve this interoperability problem through identifying and developing agreed upon standard and protocols for data sharing. These will provide the foundations of a real-estate digital market place which will comprise an ecosystem of federated platforms and digital tools and analytics. This will build off existing efforts in the establishing of the Australian Housing Data and Analytics Platform (AHDAP) funded through AURIN NCRIS.

 

EDUCATION AND TRAINING

Our education programs draw on the proven strengths of the CRC model: placing high calibre academic researchers in industry contexts to help solve complex problems. They are designed to deliver highly trained human capital and the next generation of leaders and experts equipped with the academic grounding and practical industry skills required to continue and build on the work of the CRC. Our training programs focus more on the development of materials, resources and competencies that allow partners (researchers and industry participants) to upskill and be ready for to benefit from new innovation in PropTech.

PhD Program

The PhD forms the centre of the CRCs education and training approach. It combines high-class academic research with real-world industry experiences to develop graduates capable of operating across the research-practice boundary. This approach has been proven to work effectively with other CRCs, and graduates are well equipped to progress into roles in either industry, academia or policymaking.

PhD students will be drawn from the disciplines listed above. During their research program, candidates are embedded within one or more industry partners. As such, they will have supervisory committees comprising both academic supervisors and industry supervisor. This approach has numerous benefits, including:

         Candidates receive both industry and academic experience providing a number of options for career progression and strong networks

         Ensuring research is relevant to industry, while industry benefits directly from academic expertise to specific problems

         Industry management understands the importance of contact with academia and the importance of sound methodologies in strategic decision making

In addition to this, candidates will also go through a rigorous program of core skills development that involves designing and implementing research, skills in leadership and innovation management, as well as a broad appreciation of the entre residential real estate lifecycle and the opportunities and challenges associated with its transformation.

Finally, as part of their candidacy, students will be encouraged to apply for travel to support the presentation of their research at domestic and international conferences

Postdoctoral Program

The Postdoctoral Program is designed to place well qualified resources directly within industry partners to further improve R&D capabilities as well as training and supervision of PhD candidates within partners, and to provide qualified, expert resources for our R&D projects. At the same time, postdoctoral fellows are provided with enhanced, well-rounded industry engagement experience and skills, leading to many of the same benefits and opportunities listed above. The postdoctoral program will be also industry focused with embedding of the researcher within partner organisations to ensure stronger connection from research to practice.

Training [13]

Training courses will be developed in house in conjunction with program participants. These are subject to change as programs develop, but are currently envisioned to provide courses and micro-credentials for industry, academia and policymakers covering the following opportunities:

         Up- or reskilling in specific PropTech domain areas (e.g. human centred design,, AI, property economics, data analytics)

         Deepening participant understanding of new innovations and opportunities across the residential real estate lifecycle and their implications

         Identifying and executing strategic global opportunities in existing and emerging markets including the UK, Canada, USA and India.

These will be delivered flexibly through blends of online coursework, in person hands-on technical training and seminars. Participants will also have opportunities to participate in sprints and hackathons aimed at catalysing new innovation as part of the R&D process.

Governance, Management and Assurance Structure

The corporate structure of the proposed CRC is a company limited by guarantee and constituted by members made up of the core participants in the PropTech CRC. Members are categorised as core or supporting participants depending on their contribution size.

The operation and governance of the CRC is overseen by the Board of Directors, constituted as follows:

         Board consists of 7 directors plus the Managing Director

         All director appointments are 3 year periods, after which re-appointment is subject to approval from other members

         3 Member Directors are nominated by Members of the centre, subject to the following:

o    No member may nominate or provide more than one member director

o    At least two of the three member directors must have been nominated by a member university

         4 directors are independent and are to have relevant domain experience in the CRCs area of operation

         The Chair is to be an independent director

         The CEO acts as Managing Director but does not vote on operational matters in the CRC. Experience with other CRCs suggests that it is beneficial if the CEO shares board responsibilities and liabilities. CEO tenure as Managing Director is conditional on continued employment as CEO and ends if/when no longer employed by the CRC in this capacity.

Board Committees will be formed for specific areas of activity requiring key governance oversight, namely:

         IT Security

         HR and Remuneration

Create AI powered platform utilising digital twins to create agile, dynamic urban models allowing for comprehensive scenario planning and impact assessment to inform development priorities, planning & better policy decisions

Methods

         Creation of digital twin platform entailing virtual replicas of all physical housing assets in Australia (following pilots)

         Scenario development based on criteria such as regulations, design, financial data, sustainability

         Model & analyse alternative development contribution payment systems

         Qualitative research and Case Studies documenting and analysing real-world applications and outcomes of various scenarios, e.g. zoning changes on development activities

         Utilise AI for data collection (including community feedback), consolidation and analysis to create flexible, adaptable platform

Outputs

         Platform capable of identifying potential development sites & assessing for feasibility re. zoning, planning constraints, development potential, cost benefit analysis

         Dynamic insights into local market conditions e.g. demand, property valuations, helping planners and developers make informed decisions about where/what to build to address housing needs effectively

         Tools and visualisation showing development potential and financial viability in different areas

Outcomes

         Enhanced understanding of most effective and viable planning, financial & regulatory approaches to increasing housing supply/affordability

         Data driven insights & recommendations for policymakers to support housing development and affordability

         Enhanced understanding of development impacts

         E.g. affordable housing provision (AHP) scale modelling: understand requirements and impacts of 10% AHP at LGA, Suburb, Precinct level, as well as planning and other considerations required to enable development

         E.g. 2 explore how new financial models (e.g. shared equity, built to rent) can be acknowledged in planning approval & contribution systems

         Increased housing supply stemming from superior processing & realisation of new development opportunities

 

3.2 Development Application Automation

Aim

Streamline compliance checking processes for submitted development applications using automated technologies, building off & scaling work already begun by partner Archistar (in Victoria, Canada, US)

Methods

         AI technology development assessing development proposals against digitised planning regulations (see 3.3 below)

         Mechanisms to expedite referrals from other agencies

Outputs

         Automated compliance tools, streamlined application processing systems

Outcomes

         Faster approval processes moving from months to days

         Reduced administrative burdens

         Higher compliance accuracy

 

3.3 Building & Planning Code Digitisation

Aim

Digitise and continuously update building/planning codes in conjunction with 3.2 above to create a response system that adapts to precedents, regulations and new design methods (e.g. modular design)

Methods

         Digitise physical codes and embed modular design elements for ease of application submission and code updating

         AI powered monitoring of new precedents/changes in regulations & stakeholder feedback to update real codes accordingly

Outputs

         Automated design compliance processes in line with local regulations, drastically reducing time/cost with design and approvals

Outcomes

         Responsive Planning System adapting to new precedents/regulatory changes meaning planning rules always current

         Significantly more efficient & adaptable planning and development processes, reducing supply bottlenecks and time from design to build

         Clarity and consistency in planning rules, reducing disputes related to outdated/ambiguous regulations

 

3.4 Large Language Models (LLMs) for Property Owner Guidance

Aim

Utilise LLMs to provide property owners with comprehensive guidance on navigating complex rules for property modifications/additions while ensuring rules guide but do not constrain innovative design

Methods

         Develop & train LLM on property laws, regulations, best practices

         Create interactive platform for property owners to input queries and receive clear, detailed guidance on both common (e.g. secondary dwelling, standard renovations) and rarer property modifications

         Update system in line with feedback, latest regulations & trends in property design

Outputs

         User friendly platform providing personalised advice to property owners

         Comprehensive collection of regulatory/legal/design data used to train LLM

         Engagement insights detailing how property owners use platform & types of queries they have, to help design policy and update regulations in future

Outcomes

         Increased accessibility & efficiency of property development information

         Reduction in barriers for owners looking to develop/modify properties

         Increased secondary development and increases in housing supply

         Agile and future-proofed properties stemming from guidance referencing adaptable & sustainable design

Aim

Leverage AI technologies to improve mortgage & financing experiencing by enhancing compliance, detecting fraud, offering customised loans, and verifying underwriting data efficiently.

         AI system development for continuous compliance monitoring, fraud detection and regulatory review in lending process

         Algorithms for customised loan offerings based on individual borrower profiles and preferences

         Enhance underwriting processes with AI-driven data verification and data processing (including AVMs in 2.1 above)

         Development of automated credit risk scoring, pricing and stress testing using AI/machine learning

Outputs

         AI Compliance Monitoring Tools & Fraud Detection Systems ensuring adherence to regulatory requirements and alert potential fraud

         Customised Loan platforms for borrowers

         Automated Underwriting Solutions reducing time and errors in underwriting process

Outcomes

         Strengthened Compliance and Reduced Risks in mortgage processes

4.2 Securitised Equity Capital Pools for Saving and Liquidity

Aim

Create new investment vehicles and technologies that streamline matching investor funds to projects, eg CHPs and FHBs.

Methods

         Develop securitisation risk transfer models including templates, pricing and investor management, legal and financial structures

         Blockchain-based platform for tokenisation/securitisation of property equity, allowing investors to buy and sell interests in property pools

         Engage with regulators, tech providers, market participants to structure and advocate for viable secondary market

         Educate market and potential investors about new opportunities, risks and mechanisms

Outputs

         Fully functional secondary market platform enabling the trading of property equity interests

         Tokenisation/Securitisation mechanisms for converting property equity into traceable securities

         Index Linked Notes and Derivative contracts: financial products and benchmarks facilitating investment and risk management in the housing market

Outcomes

         Reduced investor vs first home buyer competition: relationship refashioned from competition to complementarity

         Homeowners can retain dwellings while part of property equity is traded, ensuring stability and continuous occupancy, even when in financial difficulty

         Enhanced Liquidity allowing for fluid trading of property equity interests, increasing overall liquidity/flexibility and allowing investors to diversify strategies through exposure to new class of property-related assets

         Cyclical stability for Developers through derivatives market, meaning builders & developers can hedge risks and continue projects through economic cycles, increasing housing market supply and stability

 

4.3 Advancing ESG in Impact Investing

Aim

Redefine role of environmental sustainability in ESG Impact Investing in real estate with particular focus on how energy generation and efficiency can contribute to revenue streams for Community Housing Providers (CHPs) and other property owners to provide new housing financing options and paradigms

Methods

         Develop financial products for trading solar generated energy and integrate into overall funding models

         Develop business models where energy and equity contributes to revenue beyond traditional rent rolls

         Explore/advocate integration of environmental cash flows into property valuation & investment models

Outputs

         New financial instruments for energy trade, models for integrating energy cash flows into property financing

Outcomes

         Increased investment in environmentally sustainable housing, additional cash flow sources for financing housing projects, greater attractiveness of sustainable properties to investors

         Broadened Impacting Investing Scope leading to better understanding & application of ESG in real estate

         Increased revenue streams for properties through generation of solar energy, increasing financial viability - especially for CHPs meaning improved ability to provide affordable housing and maintain properties
